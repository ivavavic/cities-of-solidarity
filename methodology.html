Process Data to action

From evidence to investment.

Every Local Action Plan follows the same six-step process: rigorous, locally led, participatory and investment-oriented. The result is not a wish list — it is a costed portfolio of projects a municipality can own, sequence and finance.

1

Municipal leadership and setup

Laying the foundation for locally led, participative planning — with the tools and team to drive it.

  • Confirm the municipality's commitment to co-lead the local action planning process, launched under the Cities of Solidarity initiative to align with national and local priorities
  • Appoint the municipal focal point to champion the process, and establish a local task force of municipal departments, NGOs, civil society and refugee representatives
  • Agree on the methodology — the socio-economic survey questionnaire and analytical framework — and brief donors on the area-based, data-driven, project-focused approach
2

Context scanning

Reviewing existing city plans and gathering key data on infrastructure and services.

  • Review city development and investment plans so refugee inclusion contributes to broader local goals — and vice versa
  • Gather public spatial data and technical inputs from municipal departments to map infrastructure coverage (housing, schools, clinics, roads, utilities)
  • Identify service capacity gaps, such as staffing in schools, clinics or social care
3

Evidence and profiling

Mapping the lived experience and conducting the urban analysis that becomes the City Profile.

  • Conduct household surveys (around 100 per city) to understand refugees' socio-economic conditions and the neighbourhoods where they live
  • Map refugee insights on access to services, mobility and community participation — capturing skills and aspirations, not only needs
  • Spatialize the data to identify geographic patterns of exclusion and opportunity, then combine survey findings with infrastructure and capacity data into a neighbourhood-level City Profile that pinpoints under-served areas
4

Stakeholder workshop

Turning evidence into project ideas — together.

  • Co-host workshops with local authorities, service providers, humanitarian partners, civil society and refugee representatives
  • Review the evidence jointly and identify relevant project ideas, drawing on example action banks and municipal priorities
  • Validate direction with the mayor and departments, so the emerging portfolio has institutional backing from the start
5

Prioritization and action portfolio

Turning spatial data and local priorities into a practical, costed portfolio of projects.

  • Prioritize and define each action — location, indicative cost, timeline, expected benefits, funding source — through participatory discussion
  • Present every action in a standardized, decision-ready format: snapshot, context, implementation steps, stakeholders, targets and indicators, and scale-up logic
  • Ensure the final portfolio addresses the gaps revealed by the analysis, benefiting displaced and host populations alike
6

Endorsement, financing and implementation

Securing government endorsement, mobilizing resources and paving the way for scale-up.

  • Formalize the plan through a government-endorsed decision, clear institutional anchoring, and integration into existing planning and budget frameworks
  • Engage donors and development partners to fund implementation, leveraging the curated project portfolio
  • Strengthen local ownership by training municipal staff to maintain and update the LAP, while promoting replication in other cities through peer learning — and showcasing results to reinforce government commitment and sustain funding for the humanitarian-to-development transition

For partners Investment & scale-up logic

Locally grounded. Regionally bankable.

Although each Local Action Plan is locally grounded, similar actions across cities can be aggregated into larger inter-municipal or regional investment packages. This allows multiple municipalities to apply together — making the pipeline more attractive to larger development partners and international financial institutions that may not engage with very small stand-alone municipal projects.

Aggregation

Inter-municipal packages

Similar priorities bundled across cities — e.g. a multi-city social rental agency programme, a childcare expansion pipeline, or energy-efficient public-works packages — with shared design, joint applications and pooled preparation.

→

Financing

Development partners & IFIs

Larger, more bankable investment packages that fit the geographic focus, sectoral mandates and financing windows of development partners and international financial institutions.

Built for synergy: the approach actively seeks synergies with existing and planned development cooperation, technical assistance, financing instruments, geographic priorities and future funding windows. By identifying where local priorities connect to the mandates and financing opportunities of participating institutions, the LAPs help municipalities position selected actions for practical support and investment.
