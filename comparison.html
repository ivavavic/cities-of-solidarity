Chapter 01 Why this matters

Inclusion is becoming a municipal question.

Refugee inclusion in Moldova is moving from emergency response to municipal systems. The question is no longer whether needs exist — it is which local systems need investment, in which city. Four Protection Community Profiling rounds now make that comparison possible.

Chapter 02 The big comparative split

Self-reliance rises. Aid dependence falls. City by city.

The four cities sit on a clear gradient. Employment among respondents climbs from roughly 1 in 9 in Comrat to more than 2 in 5 in Cahul — and reliance on humanitarian assistance falls in mirror image.

Chapter 05 Housing pressure

Where the rent eats the income.

Outside Comrat, 56–67% of households spend more than half of monthly income on housing and utilities. Add informal tenure and residential churn, and Chișinău emerges as the clearest housing-pressure case.

Comrat's housing question is different

Tenure mix & quality · % of households

Comrat is a hosting city: over half of households are hosted or housed free of charge — an asset to support, not replace. Its housing agenda is repair, insulation and registered leases.

What this means: housing is where LAPs can most visibly generate shared benefits — legalizing tenancies, upgrading stock and bridging utility costs strengthen the whole local rental market, not only refugee households. Forced moves remain rare everywhere (1–3%).

Chapter 07 Health · education · childcare · information

Four systems, one enrolment logic.

Beyond housing and work, four service systems shape daily inclusion. Each has one clear, fundable fix.

Health

83–89% uninsured in every city

Bars: could not afford healthcare. Affordability barriers concentrate in Comrat — on top of chronic conditions in ~46% of households there. Insurance enrolment is the structural fix everywhere.

Education

64–77% of HHs with school-aged children have all enrolled

Where children are not enrolled, the dominant reason everywhere is preference for the Ukrainian online curriculum. Outreach must engage the curriculum question — recognition, catch-up and dual-track models — not assume capacity limits.

Childcare

≈50–71% can access childcare — a coin-flip outside Cahul

Childcare is economic infrastructure: every unavailable place keeps a parent — usually a mother — out of work. Small sub-samples (n=15–92); counts shown where n<30.

Information & digital

41% of Chișinău HHs not confident using the internet for services

Bars: no information needs reported — demand shrinks as self-reliance grows. Navigation support belongs inside existing municipal front doors, not in parallel refugee information services. Digital module measured only in Chișinău and Cahul.

Chapter 08 City-specific priority packages

From evidence to Local Action Plans.

Each city's findings translate into one coherent municipal investment package — evidence first, then the project types it justifies. And one honest note on what the data does not support.

Comrat

The Care Package

Key evidence

27.9% aged 60+ 45.6% chronic condition 14.6% unable to work 36.9% permanent stay

Priority actions

  • Community-based social care and home care for older residents
  • Health-insurance enrolment plus medication / chronic-care support
  • Targeted social assistance bridging
  • Modest housing repair-for-lease-registration scheme
Not data-supported: large-scale labour activation or job-matching infrastructure — the activation pool is small; right-size around self-employment and part-time pathways.
Bălți

The Family Package

Key evidence

63.8% HHs with children 35.7% none enrolled 43.8% single adult + deps 41.9% unstable housing

Priority actions

  • School enrolment outreach with curriculum bridging
  • Kindergarten / creche expansion
  • Childcare-linked employment activation for single parents
  • Tenure stabilization in Bălțiul Nou and Dacia
Not data-supported: permanent-integration infrastructure premised on long-term stay — return intentions (36.2%) favour investments that serve host families equally.
Chișinău

Formalization at Scale

Key evidence

66.7% housing >50% income 41.5% no tenure doc ≈1 in 3 no work contract 41% low e-service confidence

Priority actions

  • Rental-contract formalization with landlord incentives
  • Utility / housing cost bridging tied to registered leases
  • Employment formalization and skills recognition
  • One-stop service navigation with digital support in each sector
Not data-supported: basic-needs distribution systems — 15.8% report no urgent needs; the data points to systems work, not parallel aid.
Cahul

The Consolidation Package

Key evidence

40.6% employed 69.8% plan to stay 59.4% housing >50% income 83.0% uninsured

Priority actions

  • Housing affordability support, transitioning to social-housing criteria
  • Childcare expansion for working parents
  • Vocational Romanian
  • Health-insurance enrolment through employers
Not data-supported: humanitarian cash or food distribution — 2.8% aid dependence and 27.4% no-needs indicate the caseload has largely exited emergency support.

Chapter 10 Synthesis

One template will misallocate resources.

The evidence points to municipal-system investment, not parallel humanitarian systems. The right package differs by city: care in Comrat, family services in Bălți, formalization in Chișinău, and consolidation in Cahul — while shared services like insurance enrolment, Romanian language and housing support serve refugees and host communities alike.

Notes About the evidence

Source & samples

REACH/IMPACT Protection Community Profiling (PCP) rounds: Comrat N=103 households (Apr 2025), Bălți N=105 (Apr 2025), Chișinău N=532 (Oct–Nov 2025), Cahul N=106 (Jun 2026) — 846 households and 2,143 individuals in total. Aggregated data only; no personally identifiable information.

How to read the numbers

All rounds used purposive, non-probability sampling from UNHCR contact lists supplemented by field recruitment. Findings are indicative only and not statistically representative. No statistical significance testing was performed. Sub-samples under 30 are shown as counts.

Comparability

50+ indicators were harmonized across cities; each carries a comparability rating in the Comparative Indicator Matrix. Rounds are 6–14 months apart — timing effects (notably the humanitarian cash phase-down before the Cahul round) confound some comparisons and are flagged on the relevant charts. Tenure documentation uses different denominators in the 2025 vs later rounds and is marked accordingly.

Cities of Solidarity supports Moldovan municipalities to lead on refugee inclusion while strengthening services for all residents — aligning humanitarian, development and municipal systems.
